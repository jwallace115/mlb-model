Early exits account for the overwhelming majority of losses. The model's UNDER signals are fundamentally exposed to one failure mode: a starting pitcher who does not complete five innings.

### Under Losses: How Did Scoring Distribute?

In all 22 EARLY_EXIT under losses, scoring was **distributed** rather than driven by a single inning. Only 1 of 22 had max inning runs ≥ total − 1. The pattern is consistent: once an SP exits early, the offense strings together runs across multiple innings through the bullpen. This is not a "one bad inning" story — it is a "pitching staff got exposed" story.

The 4 BLOWUP_INNING losses (starters went deep, game still lost) represent different dynamics:
- **WSN@CHC 3/28**: CHC scored 4 in inning 2 despite Horton going 6.1 IP (bullpen inherited a lead situation)
- **TEX@PHI 3/29**: TEX scored 2 in 3rd then 3 in 4th — Gore went 5.1 but the middle innings ran hot
- **DET@ARI 3/31**: DET scored 5 in 3rd inning alone (both Mize and Pfaadt went 6.0 IP)
- **PIT@CIN 4/1**: Skenes went 5 innings but PIT scored 3 in 1st inning — low-line game (3.5) was vulnerable

In blowup inning losses, the single inning explained most of the damage. These are true variance events where the starter pitched well but one frame undid the bet.

### Early Exit: Severity of SP Pull

Of the 22 early exit losses, counting individual SP exits:
- **Under 3 IP**: 5 exits (most damaging — game fully exposed to bullpen)
- **3–4 IP**: 9 exits (significant exposure)
- **4–5 IP**: 13 exits (closest calls — SP pitched 4 full innings but pulled before 5th started)

The 4.0 IP threshold is particularly problematic: Bailey Ober, Joe Ryan, Lance McCullers Jr., Nathan Eovaldi, Zac Gallen, and others all reached 4 innings but could not complete the 5th. Each of these games ended in a LOSS, suggesting the model underweights the probability of SPs reaching exactly 4 but not 5 innings, especially in the early season when pitch counts are carefully managed.

### Are OVER Losses Typically Dominant Pitching?

The 4 OVER signals produced 1 CLOSE_MISS (CIN@TEX, missed by 0.5 run), 1 OPPOSITE_RESULT (PIT@NYM 0-0 through 5), and 2 wins. The sample is too small to generalize. The one notable case: PIT@NYM OVER 4.5 produced zero runs — Keller and Peterson both went deep (6.0 and 5.1 IP) in a complete pitching duel, the opposite of what the model expected.

**78.6% of losses are structural, not variance.** The model is not just getting unlucky — it has a systematic blind spot around starter durability in the early season. When SPs complete 5 innings, the UNDER hits frequently and convincingly. When they do not, the game almost always goes over the line.

### Model Calibration

The model claimed an average p_under of 0.629 for UNDER signals. Actual win rate was 44.7%. This is a meaningful gap. Even accounting for the expected break-even of ~47.6% at -110 juice, the model is underperforming its claimed probability by roughly 18 percentage points.

By confidence bucket (UNDER signals only):
- **p_under 0.57–0.60**: 28% win rate (18 games) — severely underperforming
- **p_under 0.60–0.65**: 50% win rate (18 games) — roughly break-even
- **p_under 0.65–0.70**: 67% win rate (6 games) — performing well
- **p_under 0.70–0.76**: 60% win rate (5 games) — performing near expectation

The low-confidence tier (0.57–0.60) is a drag. These signals may not clear the threshold for genuine edge.

### Opening Series (March 26–27) vs Regular Season

The Tokyo/opening series games (Mar 26–27, 14 signals) went W5 L8 P1 (-2.34u), while regular season games (Mar 29+, 38 signals) went W18 L20 (-2.93u). The opening series had a higher EARLY_EXIT rate (6/8 losses vs 16/20), consistent with teams managing pitch counts early in the season/series.

### Model Strengths
1. **Identifies elite SP matchups accurately.** When both starters complete 5+ innings in an under signal, the win rate appears well above break-even.
2. **High-confidence signals (p_under ≥ 0.65) perform near claimed probability.**
3. **Wins are decisive.** 74% of wins hit by 2+ runs; the model does not squeak by.

### Model Weaknesses
1. **No starter durability input.** The model does not appear to incorporate early-season pitch count management, injury risk, or historical early-exit rates for each pitcher. SPs completing 4.0–4.2 innings before pulling is common enough in April to require explicit modeling.
2. **Low-confidence tier is unproductive.** Signals in the 0.57–0.60 p_under range are net negative and may not warrant action.
3. **Opening series / early April exposure.** Teams manage starters conservatively in the first week. An early-season discount factor on UNDER signals may be warranted.
4. **No recovery signal.** The model fires UNDER repeatedly on the same matchup (e.g., OAK@ATL 3 games, ARI/DET 3 games) even after SP durability failures have been observed. A "recently-exited SP" flag could filter repeat-signal losses.
